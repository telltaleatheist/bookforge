## TRANSFORMATIONS — APPLY IN THIS ORDER

### STEP 1: REMOVE FOOTNOTE AND REFERENCE NUMBERS

Footnote and reference numbers are artifacts from print books. They must be DELETED — never converted to words.

**What footnote numbers look like:**
- Bracketed: [1], [23], (1), (23) → DELETE entirely
- Glued to punctuation with no space: "said.13 The" → "said. The"
- After punctuation with a space: "...the end. 3 The next" → "...the end. The next"
- At the very end of a paragraph: "...some conclusion.7" → "...some conclusion."

**How to tell a footnote number from real content:**

A number is a FOOTNOTE if ALL of these are true:
1. It is a small number (typically 1–200)
2. It appears immediately after punctuation (touching it or one space after)
3. Removing it makes the text read MORE naturally, not less
4. It is NOT part of a phrase like "chapter 3," "the 5 men," "12 years," or "World War 2"

A number is REAL CONTENT if ANY of these are true:
- It is part of a noun phrase: "the 3 survivors," "all 50 states"
- It follows a verb or preposition: "counted 12," "about 7 miles"
- It is a year, age, measurement, or quantity that makes sense in context
- Removing it would make the sentence incomplete or nonsensical

**If you are unsure whether a number is a footnote, LEAVE IT.**

**Example:**

INPUT: The panic spread rapidly.13 In Los Angeles, children said that teachers had molested them.14 Similar accusations arose in other cities. 15
OUTPUT: The panic spread rapidly. In Los Angeles, children said that teachers had molested them. Similar accusations arose in other cities.

### STEP 2: REMOVE PAGE ARTIFACTS

These are leftover artifacts from PDF-to-ebook conversion:
- Page numbers (standalone numbers like "47" on their own line, or "Page 7")
- Running headers/footers (book title, chapter name, or author name, often in ALL CAPS or with unusual letter spacing, appearing mid-paragraph)
- Stray single letters or fragments that clearly don't belong

When you remove a page artifact that splits a paragraph, rejoin the paragraph into one continuous block.

**Example:**

INPUT:
This second edition follows the structure of the first. There is one
new chapter, two prior chapters that merged, and

N E O N A Z I S  I N  A M E R I C A     7

two new charts at the back that attempt to assess the impact.

OUTPUT:
This second edition follows the structure of the first. There is one new chapter, two prior chapters that merged, and two new charts at the back that attempt to assess the impact.

---

### STEP 3: FIX OCR ERRORS

Only fix errors where you are confident of the correct word:
- Broken hyphenation across lines: "recon-\nstruct" → "reconstruct"
- Common character misreads: "rn" → "m", "cl" → "d" — but ONLY when the result is a real word in context (for example, "srnall" → "small")
- Words split by spaces: "psy chiatry" → "psychiatry"
- Duplicate adjacent words: "the the" → "the"

If you are not sure a word is an OCR error, leave it unchanged.

---

### STEP 4: FIX DECORATIVE LETTER SPACING

PDF conversion sometimes spaces out letters for visual effect. Collapse them:
- Spaced-out letters: "B O N H O E F F E R" → "BONHOEFFER"
- Spaced-out numbers: "1 9 0 6" → "1906"
- Mixed: "C H A P T E R  I V" → "CHAPTER IV"

**Pattern:** Three or more single characters separated by single spaces is almost always decorative spacing. Collapse them into one word.

---

### STEP 5: CONVERT NUMBERS TO SPOKEN WORDS

Now that footnote numbers have been removed in Step 1, convert remaining content numbers to how a narrator would say them aloud:
- Years: "1923" → "nineteen twenty-three"
- Decades: "the 1930s" → "the nineteen thirties"
- Ordinals: "1st" → "first", "21st" → "twenty-first"
- Cardinals: "3 men" → "three men"
- Currency: "$5.50" → "five dollars and fifty cents"
- Percentages: "40%" → "forty percent"
- Roman numerals (names/titles): "Henry VIII" → "Henry the Eighth"
- Roman numerals (chapters/parts): "Chapter IV" → "Chapter Four"

**Leave unchanged:** Phone numbers, long ID numbers, or any number you are unsure how to pronounce naturally.

---

### STEP 6: FORMAT LISTS FOR TTS

Lists need punctuation so TTS engines read them with proper pauses.

**Numbered lists** — convert the number to a spoken word, add a period after it, and ensure the item ends with a period:

INPUT:
1. Get milk from the store
2. Go to the church

OUTPUT:
One. Get milk from the store.
Two. Go to the church.

**Lettered lists** — same pattern:

INPUT:
(a) The first argument
(b) The second argument

OUTPUT:
A. The first argument.
B. The second argument.

**Bulleted/unordered lists** — remove the bullet and ensure each item ends with a period:

INPUT:
- Faster processing speed
- Lower memory usage

OUTPUT:
Faster processing speed.
Lower memory usage.

---

### STEP 7: EXPAND ABBREVIATIONS

Expand abbreviations so TTS pronounces them correctly:
- "Mr." → "Mister"
- "Mrs." → "Missus"
- "Dr." → "Doctor"
- "Prof." → "Professor"
- "St." (before a name) → "Saint"
- "e.g." → "for example"
- "i.e." → "that is"
- "etc." → "and so on"
- "vs." → "versus"
- "approx." → "approximately"

Only expand abbreviations you are confident about. If "St." could mean "Street" or "Saint," use context to decide. If unsure, leave it.
